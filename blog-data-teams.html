Data Teams Look Very Different Than They Did 5 Years Ago

The shift from large departments to lean, infrastructure-focused pods.

The modern data team has completely fundamentally transformed in just a half-decade.

If you walked into an enterprise data department five years ago, you'd likely see the "old model" in full swing: A massive 10 to 15 person team. A VP dictating top-down roadmaps. Multiple layers of middle managers. Siloed roles consisting of pure data engineers, secluded analysts, and specialized BI developers. You would submit a Jira ticket for a supposedly simple dashboard metric, and then wait three weeks to see it.

To be fair, it worked. But it was agonizingly slow, incredibly expensive, and architecturally built for a world where human beings were the only consumers of the final data output.

That world is gone.

The New Playbook

Today, the most effective, forward-thinking organizations are adopting a radically different approach to data management. The new model looks much more like this:

  • Extremely lean: 1 to 3 core practitioners, not 15.
  • Embedded leadership: A strategic leader who is actually hands-on with the codebase, not just managing ticket queues.
  • Direct partnerships: Deep collaboration with business stakeholders instead of tossing requirements over the wall.
  • Self-service analytics: Empowering marketing, sales, and operations teams to pull their own insights so they aren't held hostage waiting on IT reports.
  • Agentic Architecture: Data infrastructure designed from the ground up to be consumed by AI agents and LLMs, not just static BI dashboards.

Smarter, Not Bigger

The companies getting this right aren't focused on building massive in-house data teams with extensive payroll overhead. Instead, they are building leaner, fundamentally smarter teams. When they hit technical resource gaps, they lean heavily on fractional expertise to build out their medallion architectures or semantic layers exactly when they need it—without the bloated full-time salaries.

Why is this shift happening? Because AI is fundamentally changing the core mandate of what data teams actually do. Time spent manually building out ad-hoc reports is plummeting. Conversely, time spent building rigid, trusted, scalable foundations that allow AI agents to securely interact with enterprise data is skyrocketing. The heavy lifting is being outsourced to the intelligence layer.

The relevant question for technical founders and data leaders is no longer: "How many data people do we need?"

Instead, you must ask: "Do we have the right data infrastructure to let a small team—and our AI systems—do the work of many?"

That is the definitive shift in the modern data landscape. And it's happening faster than anyone predicted.
