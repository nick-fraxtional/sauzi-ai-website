Monday Metrics: Retention Rate

The metric that separates growing companies from leaky buckets — and why a single number almost always lies.

Retention is the metric that separates growing companies from leaky buckets. But if you're only tracking a single retention number, you're missing the story entirely.

A company with 70% retention and a company with 70% retention can be in completely different situations — depending on which customers are staying, when they're leaving, and whether retention is trending up or down with newer cohorts. That's why the real insight isn't in the headline number. It's in the cohorts.

What Is Cohort Retention?

Instead of looking at all customers together, cohort retention groups them by when they started — then tracks how each group retains over time.

This lets you see patterns you'd completely miss with a blended average:

  • Is retention improving with newer cohorts?
  • Where's the biggest drop-off happening — Month 1? Month 3?
  • Did something change in a specific month that hurt or helped retention?
  • Which acquisition channels produce customers who actually stick?

How to Read a Cohort Chart

The structure is simple once you know what you're looking at:

  • Each row is a group of customers who started in the same month
  • Each column shows the percentage still active after X months
  • Darker cells = higher retention (better)
  • Read down the columns to see if newer cohorts outperform older ones
  • Read across the rows to see where you're losing people

The Retention Curve

Almost every product sees the same shape: a steep drop in Month 1, then a gradual decline that eventually flattens out. That flat part — the stabilization point — is your core retained base. The two levers you have are:

  • Make the Month 1 drop smaller (better onboarding, faster time-to-value)
  • Make the flat part higher (stronger product-market fit, better engagement loops)

Both require clean data to even measure correctly. Which brings us to where this usually breaks down.

Where Cohort Retention Breaks Down

Beautiful cohort charts mean nothing if the underlying data is broken. And the data breaks in three predictable ways:

  • Cohort assignment: When does a customer actually "start"? First purchase? Sign-up date? Activation event? Different definitions produce completely different retention curves — and entirely different strategic conclusions.
  • Identity resolution: If the same person exists as three accounts in your database, they might show up in three different cohorts. Your data is lying to you about who's retaining and who's churning.
  • Event tracking gaps: Missing events equal missing users. Your platform stops seeing a customer's activity — not because they left, but because your tracking broke. You think they churned. They didn't. Your churn rate is artificially inflated.

The Real Problem

Most companies don't have a retention problem. They have a data problem dressed up as a retention problem.

You can hire retention consultants, run A/B tests, redesign your onboarding — and get zero signal — if the cohort data feeding those decisions is unreliable. Every fix you apply is built on a foundation that's already cracked.

The fix starts upstream: clean event tracking, consistent identity resolution, a defined and documented definition of "when a customer starts." Once those are in place, the cohort chart stops being a source of confusion and starts being one of the most powerful tools in your business.
