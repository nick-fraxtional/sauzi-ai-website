Monday Metrics: Weeks of Cover

The early warning system for stockouts and overstocking — and why your number is probably already wrong by the time you see it.

If you sell physical products, Weeks of Cover is the metric that determines whether you're growing or firefighting. Get it right and you never run out of your best-sellers. Get it wrong and you're either sitting on dead inventory or turning away customers during your biggest sales periods.

The formula looks simple. The execution almost always isn't.

What It Is

Weeks of Cover (WoC) tells you how many weeks your current inventory will last at the current rate of sales.

WoC = Current Inventory ÷ Average Weekly Sell-Through

A number above your reorder lead time means you're covered. A number below it means you're at risk of a stockout. Simple in theory — but here's where it falls apart in practice.

Four Ways WoC Breaks Down

1. Inventory lag. If your warehouse counts update weekly — or worse, monthly — your WoC is already stale by the time it hits your dashboard. A product that shows "6 weeks of cover" on Monday might have 3.5 by Friday after a weekend sales spike. Real-time inventory sync isn't a nice-to-have. It's the baseline requirement for this metric to mean anything.

2. Demand averaging. A flat average weekly sell-through ignores seasonality entirely. Your "6 weeks of cover" in January means nothing if February demand spikes 3x because of Valentine's Day or a promotional event. At minimum, use a trailing 4-week weighted average. Better yet, layer in seasonal adjustment factors.

3. SKU granularity. Measuring WoC at the category level is where most companies go wrong. One hot SKU can mask ten dead ones. You think "Apparel" has 6 weeks of cover — but your best-selling jacket has 0.8 weeks and your slowest polo has 40. Stockouts and overstock exist in the same category simultaneously, and a blended number hides both.

4. The heatmap tells the story. When you visualize WoC across categories and time — a grid of SKUs by month, colored from green (healthy) to red (critical) — you can see trouble coming months before it hits. Red cells aren't just data points. They're revenue at risk. The heatmap turns a spreadsheet metric into a decision-making tool.

What Good Looks Like

  • WoC calculated at the SKU level, not category
  • Inventory refreshed daily or in real-time, not weekly
  • Demand averages use a weighted trailing window with seasonal adjustment
  • WoC threshold alerts set at the reorder lead time + buffer
  • Heatmap visualization surfaced automatically — no manual refresh needed

The Real Problem

Most companies know they need to track Weeks of Cover. The problem isn't the formula. The problem is that the inputs — inventory counts and sell-through rates — are sitting in three different systems that don't talk to each other, updating at different frequencies, with no single source of truth.

If your inventory data isn't clean and real-time, your Weeks of Cover is a guess, not a strategy.
