Planning an AI Project? Ask These 5 Questions First.

Before you invest in AI tooling, models, or consultants—take 60 seconds and be honest with yourself.

Every executive wants to implement AI right now. The pressure from the board is real, and the competitive landscape demands it. But before you invest in shiny new AI tooling, sign a massive contract for proprietary models, or bring in expensive consultants—take 60 seconds and be completely honest about your foundation.

AI is simply an accelerator. If you point it at clean, structured data, it accelerates insight. If you point it at a disorganized swamp, it accelerates confusion and hallucinations.

1. Do you trust your current dashboards?

The Symptom: Different departments show up to leadership meetings with reports that pull from the "same" system, yet display wildly different numbers for core metrics like Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR) or Active Users.

The Reality: If humans looking at your BI tools can't agree on what reality looks like, an LLM won't be able to either. AI models lack the nuanced, historical context that "Bob in finance excludes these specific legacy accounts when pulling his report." To an AI, inconsistent data simply looks like contradictory facts.

2. How long does a "simple" data question take?

The Symptom: When the CEO asks a straightforward question like "How did this sub-segment perform compared to last quarter?", the response is "Let me file a Jira ticket for the data team, they'll get back to us on Thursday."

The Reality: If the answer is "days," "it depends who you ask," or "we need to join these three spreadsheets manually," that's a massive red flag. Your data isn't modeled for self-service answering. An AI agent trying to perform Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) against your databases will hit the exact same structural bottlenecks your human analysts do.

3. Is there a single source of truth for key metrics?

The Symptom: Sales tracks "churn" as a canceled contract. Customer Success tracks "churn" as a lack of login activity for 30 days. Finance tracks "churn" only when the final invoice is unpaid.

The Reality: Revenue, churn, customer count—does everyone explicitly agree on these definitions, and is that agreement codified in code? This is where a semantic layer comes in. If an AI agent attempts to calculate a metric without a clearly defined, globally accepted formula, the outputs will be fundamentally flawed and untrustworthy.

4. Could an outsider understand your data schema?

The Symptom: You have tables named `tbl_cust_data_v4_FINAL` with fifty poorly named columns like `flag_1` and `dt_mod` that only make sense to the original developer who built the database in 2018.

The Reality: LLMs are incredibly adept at writing SQL—but only if the database schema is logically structured, properly aliased, and well-documented. If your data ecosystem requires deep institutional knowledge to navigate, an AI won't be able to figure it out either. You need clean semantic mapping, descriptions, and logical foreign keys.

5. Do your pipelines run reliably without intervention?

The Symptom: A data engineer has to log in every Tuesday at 8 AM to manually re-run a script because the API connection to your CRM times out sporadically.

The Reality: AI agents and automated workflows require consistent, fresh, and reliable data to function autonomously. Broken pipelines equal broken AI. You cannot build a durable AI solution on top of brittle infrastructure that requires constant human life support.

If you answered "no" or "I don't know" to most of these—you're not alone. The vast majority of companies are rushing toward generative AI without having solved traditional Business Intelligence first.

But here's the good news: this is entirely fixable. And contrary to popular belief, modernizing this foundation doesn't necessarily take an 18-month migration project or a massive 15-person engineering team.

How many of these did you answer "yes" to? Be honest.
